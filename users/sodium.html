Ξ  Disorder of Sodium Regulation

Hypernatremia (Na+ > 150 mEq/L)

Pathophysiology of Hypernatremia

  • As hypernatremia develops, the brain generates idiogenic osmoles to increase the intracellular osmolality and prevent the loss of brain water. This mechanism is not instantaneous and is most prominent when hypernatremia has developed gradually. If the serum sodium concentration is lowered rapidly, there is movement of water from the serum into the brain cells to equalize the osmolality in the 2 compartments. The resultant brain swelling manifests as seizures or coma.

  • Acute, severe hypernatremia, usually secondary to sodium administration, can be corrected more rapidly because idiogenic osmoles have not had time to accumulate. This balances the high morbidity and mortality rates associated with hypernatremia with the dangers of overly rapid correction. When hypernatremia is severe and is due to sodium intoxication, it may be impossible to administer enough water to correct the hypernatremia rapidly without worsening the volume overload. In this situation, peritoneal dialysis allows for removal of the excess sodium. This requires dialysis fluid with a high glucose concentration and a low sodium concentrationg) Monitor patient clinically & serum sodium q 4 hrs until stable trend
  • Degree of hypovolemia may be underestimated based only on physical examination findings (loss of skin turgor, shrunken eyes). Patient usually maintains blood pressure and urine output. Because of hypernatremia, intravascular volume is well preserved (because of the shift of water from the intracellular space to the extracellular space.) and dehydration is mostly intracellular.
  • Monitor glucose. Hyperglycemia with hypernatremia is not usually treated with insulin because the acute decrease in glucose may precipitate cerebral edema by lowering plasma osmolality (glucose become important osmole in hyperglycemia). Rather, plasma glucose should be reduced slowly by reducing glucose concentration in IV fluids).
  • The secondary hypocalcemia is treated as needed (hypernatremia may be associated with hypocalcemia).

Causes of Hypernatremia

a. Euvolemic Hypernatremia (decrease in ICF space with normal ECF space)

  1. Iatrogenic, intravenous fluids or medications.
  2. Increased insensible losses (skin, respiratory eg. Fever).
  3. Decreased H2O intake, Ineffective breast-feeding, child neglect.

b. Hypovolemic Hypernatremia (decreased in ECF & ICF spaces)

  1. Increased extra-renal losses (urine [Na+] < 20 mEq/L).
  2. Skin: phototherapy, burns, sweating premature infants and radiant warmer.
  3. GI: AGE, diarrhea, hypertonic enemas, or poor intake.
  4. Increased renal losses (urine [Na+] > 20 mEq/L).
  5. Central or Nephrogenic DI.
  6. Diabetes mellitus, osmotic diuresis (mannitol).
  7. Renal dysplasia, obstructive uropathy (once obstruction release), Polyuric phase of acute tubular necrosis.

c. Hypervolemic Hypernatremia (decreased ICF & increased ECF space)

  1. “Salt poisoning” urine [Na+] > 20 mEq/L. Ingestion of seawater or sodium chloride.
  2. Hypertonic salt solutions (mixing errors of formula or oral rehydration solutions, steroid excess, IV hypertonic NaHCO3/ 3% NaCl or hypertonic dialysis.
  3. Primary hyperaldosteronism (associated hypertension and hypokalemia).

Clinical Manifestations of Hypernatremia

Hypernatremia resulting from water loss occurs only if the patient does not have access to water or is unable to drink. Severe sodium intoxication causes signs of volume overload, such as pulmonary edema and weight gain. Salt poisoning is associated with an elevated fractional excretion of sodium, whereas hypernatremic dehydration causes a low fractional excretion of sodium.

  1. CNS: Apathy, irritability, tremors, altered mental status, seizures (more after rapid correction), restlessness, muscle weakness, high-pitched cry and hyperpnea. Alert patients are very thirsty. It can cause brain shrinkage, resulting in vascular rupture and intracranial bleeding (specially premature infants). Even though central pontine myelinolysis (CPM) is classically associated with overly rapid correction of hyponatremia, both CPM and extrapontine myelinolysis can occur in children with hypernatremia. Thrombotic complications occur in severe hypernatremic dehydration and include stroke, dural sinus thrombosis, peripheral thrombosis, and renal vein thrombosis.
  2. GI: Anorexia, thirst, vomiting, diarrhea.
  3. SKIN: Doughy skin.
  4. Hypernatremia is associated with hyperglycemia and mild hypocalcemia; the mechanisms are unknown.

Treatment of Hypernatremia

In general, the more dilute the replacement fluid that is being used to correct the free water deficit, the SLOWER it should be infused. The majority of pediatric intensivists will rehydrate hypernatremic patients with a crystalloid solution no more hypotonic than 0.45% NS (1/2 NS). Fluid more hypotonic than 1/4 NS should be avoided as it may cause lyses of cells and lead to cerebral edema. Seizures are the most common manifestation of cerebral edema and result from an overly rapid decrease of the serum sodium concentration during correction of hypernatremic dehydration. Acutely, increasing the serum concentration via an infusion of 3% sodium chloride can reverse the cerebral edema.

Treatment of Hypernatremic Dehydration

Restore intravascular volume

  • Normal saline: 20 mL/kg over 20 min if patient is in shock(Repeat until intravascular volume restored).

Determine the time for correction based on the initial sodium concentration

  • [Na]: 145-157 mEq/L: 24 hr.
  • [Na]: 158-170 mEq/L: 48 hr.
  • [Na]: 171-183 mEq/L: 72 hr.
  • [Na]: 184-196 mEq/L: 84 hr.

Administer fluid at a constant rate over the time for correction

  • Typical fluids: D5 ¼ NS OR D5 1/2 NS (Both with 20 mEq/L KCl unless contraindicated).
  • Typical rate: 1.25-1.5 times maintenance.
  • Remember 0.9% NS has 154 mEq/L of Na which will decrease the serum Na if > 154.

Follow serum sodium concentration Adjust fluid based on clinical status and serum sodium concentration

  • Signs of volume depletion: administer NS (20 mL/kg).

If sodium falls too rapidly,

  • Increase sodium concentration of intravenous fluid, or
  • Decrease rate of intravenous fluid.

If sodium falls too slowly,

  • Decrease sodium concentration of intravenous fluid, or
  • Increase rate of intravenous fluid.

Rate of decrease should not exceed 0.5-1 mEq/L/hr or 10 mEq/L per day.

Hyponatremia (Na+ < 135 mEq/L)

Pathophysiology of Hyponatremia

Sodium is a major solute in the blood and major determinant of osmolarity. Hyponatremia is mainly disease of osmolarity. If plasma osmolarity is maintained patient may not show any symptoms.

Calculated POsm = 2Na + (Glucose/18) + (BUN/2.8)

Hyponatremia causes a decrease in the osmolality of the extracellular space. Because of this water inevitably moves from the extracellular space to the intracellular space to maintain osmotic equilibrium. The increase in intracellular water causes cells to swell. It is potentially dangerous in the brain because it is contained in a fixed shell, the skull. As brain cells swell, there is an increase in intracranial pressure. Acute, severe hyponatremia can cause brainstem herniation as there is no time for cells to adapt changing osmoloarity. Manifestations are significantly less if the hyponatremia develops gradually because the brain adapts to the decreased extracellular osmolality by decreasing its internal osmolality. Initially, this is mostly through loss of sodium, potassium, and chloride. More chronically, there is loss of intracellular osmoles, such as amino acids.

Causes of Hyponatremia

  1. Pseudohyponatremia: serum sodium is low but osmolarity is either high (mannitol, glycerol and hyperglycemia) or normal (hyper-proteinemia, hyperlipidemia).
  2. True Hyponatremia.

  1. Hypovolemic Hyponatremia(sodium loss has been higher than water loss)

  • Renal losses, (urine [Na+] > 20 mEq/L): diuretic excess, osmotic diuresis, obstructive uropathy, adrenal insufficiency, Fanconi syndrome, Bartter’s syndrome, interstitial nephritis, RTA (bicarb. loss), cerebral salt wasting, Polyuric phase of acute tubular necrosis.
  • GI losses, (urine [Na+] < 20 mEq/L): vomiting, diarrhea, fistula, post-op tubes, gastrocystoplasty.
  • Sweat, (urine [Na+] < 20 mEq/L): CF (hyponatremic, hypochloremic, metabolic alkalosis), heat stroke.
  • Third space, (urine [Na+] < 20 mEq/L): effusions, ascites, burns, muscle trauma, pancreatitis, peritonitis.

  2. Euvolemic, Hypotonic (water retention with no gain or loss of Na, Urine Na>20)

  • Water intoxication: IVF's (hypotonic fluid), tap water enema, psychogenic water drinking, diluted formula feedings (WIC Syndrome), and Beer potomania.
  • ADH excess (unable to excrete water): SIADH , pain, drugs (MSO4, cytoxan, vincristine, TCA's, ASA, indomethacin, etc.).
  • Glucocorticoid deficiency.
  • Hypothyroidism.
  • Reset osmostat: CVA, infection (TB), malnutrition.

  3. Hypervolemic, Hypotonic (net retention of sodium and water)

  • Edematous states, (urine [Na+] < 20 mEq/L): CHF, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome, Capillary leak due to sepsis, Hypoalbuminemia due to gastrointestinal disease (protein-losing enteropathy).
  • Renal failure, (urine [Na+] > 20 mEq/L: acute and chronic Renal failure.

Diagnostic Criteria for Syndrome of Inappropriate Antidiuretic Hormone

  • Absence of
    • Renal, adrenal, or thyroid insufficiency.
    • Congestive heart failure, nephrotic syndrome, or cirrhosis.
    • Diuretic ingestion.
    • Dehydration.
  • Urine osmolality > 100 (usually > plasma).
  • Serum osmolality < 280 and serum sodium < 135.
  • Urine sodium > 25.

Clinical Manifestations of Hyponatremia

  1. Pseudohuponatremia – patient will be asymptomatic.
  2. True Hyponatremia
    • Symptoms depend on degree of hypotonicity, whether it developed rapidly or slowly & the age of the patient.
    • Symptoms: Apathy, agitation, anorexia, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, weakness, hypothermia, altered mental status, coma, hypotension, seizures. Patients may have hypothermia and Cheyne-Stokes respirations. Hyponatremia can cause muscle cramps and weakness.
    • Hypothermic infant with seizures most likely has acute hyponatremia.
    • Patients with hyponatremic dehydration have more manifestations of intravascular volume depletion than patients who have equivalent water loss but normal or increased sodium concentrations.

Treatment of Hyponatremia

If patient is in shock, give isotonic fluid boluses, till shock is corrected irrespective of sodium value.

Symptomatic Hyponatremia

  • Acute decrease in Na+ to < 120 mEq/L.
  • Clinical CNS symptoms → seizures, coma & often hypothermia.
  • Symptoms usually resolve when Na+ increased by 3-5 mEq/L. Intravenous hypertonic saline rapidly increases serum sodium, and the effect on serum osmolality leads to a decrease in brain edema. Each mL/kg of 3% sodium chloride increases the serum sodium by approximately 1 mEq/L. A child with active symptoms often improves after receiving 4–6 mL/kg of 3% sodium chloride over 30 to 90 min. Repeat dose if symptoms persist.

Acute Asymptomatic Hyponatremia

  1. Hypovolemic states are treated with the correction of shock/ECF deficit with aggressive isotonic crystalloid replacement.
  2. Euvolemic states are treated with water restriction. In isovolumic hyponatremia, there is usually an excess of water and a mild sodium deficit. Therapy is directed at eliminating the excess water. Specific hormone replacement is the cornerstone of therapy for the hyponatremia of hypothyroidism or cortisol deficiency. SIADH is a condition of excess water, with limited ability of the kidney to excrete water. The mainstay of therapy is fluid restriction. Therapy with saline can create problems in children with SIADH. Infusions of saline temporarily increase the sodium concentration, but increase the child's blood pressure. This causes the kidneys to eliminate almost all of the sodium, eliminating any benefit from the therapy. The water contained in the saline is retained; thus, the hyponatremia may actually worsen. Furosemide is more successful in the patient with SIADH and severe hyponatremia. Even in a patient with SIADH, furosemide causes an increase in water and sodium excretion. The loss of sodium is somewhat counterproductive, but this sodium can be replaced with hypertonic saline. Conivaptan, a V2-receptor antagonist, decreases the permeability of the collecting duct to water producing an aquaresis. It is approved for short-term treatment of euvolemic patients with hyponatremia (usually SIADH).
  3. Hypervolemic states are corrected with water restriction and may require Na restriction as well. Diuretics may help by causing excretion of both sodium and water. Patients with low albumin due to nephrotic syndrome have a better response to diuretics after an infusion of albumin; the sodium concentration often normalizes due to expansion of the intravascular volume. The patient with renal failure cannot respond to any of these therapies except fluid restriction. A more definitive approach in children with renal failure is to perform dialysis, which removes water and sodium.

Chronic (> 48 hours) Asymptomatic Hyponatremia

  1. Correct hyponatremia slowly, no faster than 0.6-1 mEq/L/hr.
  2. Rapid correction may cause osmotic demyelination syndrome.
  3. Correct deficit over 24-48 hours, Do not over correct..
  4. Follow electrolytes every 4 hours to monitor for overly rapid correction.
